MY WORK

  • Cargo ships create a lot of noise as their huge engines churn across the surface of the ocean. This noise travels through the ocean environment, changing subtly as it passes through shifts in water temperature, penetrates the seabed, or reflects off the seafloor. Geoacoustic inversion is the challenge of reverse-engineering the condition of the ocean environment based on recorded underwater sounds. For my thesis work, I studied how spectrograms from the noise of passing ships might be used to perform geoacoustic inversion and estimate the properties of the seabed with ResNet-18, a deep network. Since it is difficult (and expensive!) to gather acoustic data from the ocean, my project leveraged a computational model called ORCA to generate synthetic data for training. I developed a robust module in Python for analyzing parameter uncertainty and model sensitivity for ORCA, including sophisticated finite-differencing machinery under the hood.

Deep learning models such as the convolutional neural network (CNN) are powerful tools for extracting detailed patterns from complex images. In this project, I trained a small CNN on slide images of cells to identify the portions of those images that contained cancerous cells.   • I am not much of a painter myself, but I love the way some painters use texture to develop a personal style for their art. For this project, I used a technique called style transfer, introduced by Gatys et al. in their paper “A Neural Algorithm of Artistic Style” (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1508.06576.pdf) to transform a photograph of a pumpkin so that it looked like it was a picture of a pumpkin painted by Vincent Van Gogh in the style of is famous “Starry Night”. Mathematically, this project amounted to projecting the pumpkin image into the style space of the “Starry Night” with a Gram Matrix.
